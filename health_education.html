Learn how to protect your health and take action for cleaner air

Understanding Air Pollution

Air pollution is a complex mix of gases and particles that harm both health and the planet. NASA, WHO, and EPA study these pollutants to protect public health and guide environmental policy. The main pollutants include:

  • Nitrogen Dioxide (NO₂): From vehicles and industry; causes lung irritation and contributes to smog.
  • Particulate Matter (PM₂.₅): Tiny dust and smoke particles; increases risks of heart and lung disease.
  • Ozone (O₃): Created by sunlight reacting with pollutants; causes chest pain and breathing issues.
  • Formaldehyde (HCHO): From fires and industry; irritant and carcinogen with long-term exposure.
  • Carbon Monoxide (CO): From burning fuels; reduces oxygen delivery in the blood.
  • Aerosols: Microscopic particles from dust, fires, and pollution; influence air quality and climate.

Learn more at WHO: Outdoor Air Quality and Health.

Protecting Yourself and Your Family

Practical steps to stay healthy when air quality declines:

  • Check local air quality forecasts daily using AirNow.gov or your weather app.
  • Stay indoors during high pollution days, especially for sensitive groups.
  • Use HEPA air purifiers and seal windows when air is bad.
  • Avoid outdoor exercise when ozone levels are high (afternoons in summer).
  • Wear a properly fitted N95 mask during wildfire smoke or heavy haze.
  • Do not burn trash or use unvented stoves indoors.

Improving Air Quality in Your Community
  • Support renewable energy and local clean air initiatives.
  • Reduce driving; carpool, bike, or use public transit when possible.
  • Plant trees and maintain green areas to help filter pollutants.
  • Conserve electricity — many power plants emit air pollutants.
  • Encourage schools and businesses to track air quality indoors and outdoors.

When to Seek Medical Help

Pollution exposure can trigger serious health effects. Seek medical attention if you experience:

  • Shortness of breath or wheezing during pollution events
  • Chest pain or heart palpitations
  • Severe headaches, dizziness, or fatigue
  • Asthma attacks or prolonged coughing

Visit your local emergency department or call for medical help if symptoms worsen.
